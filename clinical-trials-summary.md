## Trial 1 — Neuromuscular Intervention Targeted to Mechanisms of ACL Load in Female Athletes {#trial-1}

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | [NCT03190889](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT03190889) |
| **Status** | ✅ Completed |
| **Sponsor** | Mayo Clinic (NIH/NIAMS-funded: R01AR055563) |
| **PI** | Aaron J. Krych, MD |
| **Study Dates** | August 2018 – June 2024 |
| **Enrollment** | 150 participants (ages 13–30; ~58% female) |
| **Design** | Prospective, randomized, double-blind, parallel-arm RCT |
| **Locations** | Mayo Clinic (Rochester & Minneapolis, MN), Ohio State University, Emory University |

### Background & Rationale
Nearly **1 in 3 athletes** who sustain a primary ACL injury and return to sport will experience a secondary ACL injury. Female athletes face a disproportionately higher risk of **contralateral** re-injury, while males are more prone to ipsilateral graft failure. This trial aimed to:
1. Stratify patients into risk groups based on movement biomechanics and neuromuscular performance.
2. Test whether **Targeted Neuromuscular Training (TNMT)** reduces second ACL injury rates versus standard care.

### Study Arms

| Arm | Description |
|---|---|
| **TNMT (Experimental)** | 12 supervised PT sessions over 6 weeks. Core & hip strengthening, neuromuscular training with verbal/visual biofeedback, single-leg drills on both limbs. |
| **STAN (Standard of Care)** | 12 supervised PT sessions over 6 weeks. Agility/plyometric drills + home running program. No movement-quality feedback. |
| **HOME (Control)** | Home-only program: running + strengthening twice/week for 6 weeks. No plyometrics, no neuromuscular training. |

### Key Outcomes

**Primary Outcome — Second ACL Injury Rate (12–48 month follow-up):**

| Group | Participants Tracked | Second ACL Injuries | Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| STAN | 27 | 3 | **11.1%** |
| TNMT | 32 | 5 | **15.6%** |
| HOME | 28 | 7 | **25.0%** |

> 📌 **Notable Finding:** The HOME group had the highest re-injury rate (25%), underscoring the risk of minimal supervised rehabilitation. STAN and TNMT showed comparable primary outcomes, though secondary biomechanical data (frontal plane kinetics/kinematics) are anticipated for posting by December 2027.

**Secondary Outcomes (Pending full publication):**
- Frontal plane knee kinetics (joint moments during athletic tasks)
- Frontal plane knee kinematics (joint excursion and angle during athletic tasks)
- Measured before and after 6 weeks of intervention

### Rehabilitation Protocol Highlights
- **Pre-op rehab** standardized for all participants before surgery
- Post-ACLR, patients progress through phases culminating in sport-specific training
- TNMT protocol targets: core/hip strengthening, movement pattern correction, biofeedback-guided drills
- Predictive biomechanical risk factors for re-injury include: hip internal rotation moment, frontal plane knee movement, asymmetric knee extensor moment at initial contact, single-leg postural stability deficits

### Eligibility Summary
- Ages 13–30, first-time isolated ACL injury (<6 months prior)
- Pre-injury participation in cutting/jumping/pivoting sports ≥50 hrs/year
- No prior knee surgery, no direct-blow mechanism, no complex meniscus tears

## Trial 2 — HMB Supplementation on Post-Operative Muscle Mass and Function in Female Athletes {#trial-2}

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | [NCT05869812](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT05869812) |
| **Acronym** | FAKS-HMB |
| **Status** | 🔄 Actively Recruiting |
| **Sponsor** | University of Kansas Medical Center |
| **PI** | Ashley A. Herda, Ph.D., CSCS*D |
| **Study Dates** | May 2023 – December 2028 (est.) |
| **Enrollment** | 30 female participants (ages 18–45) |
| **Phase** | Phase 2 |
| **Design** | Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled parallel RCT |
| **Location** | University of Kansas Health System, Overland Park, KS |

### Background & Rationale
Post-operative **disuse atrophy** of the quadriceps and hamstrings is a primary barrier to return-to-sport after ACL reconstruction. Female athletes are uniquely susceptible to significant injury due to hormonal, anatomical, and biomechanical predispositions. This trial tests whether **Calcium β-hydroxy-β-methylbutyrate (CaHMB)** — a supplement shown to enhance muscle protein synthesis and reduce atrophy during bed rest — can preserve and accelerate recovery of muscle mass and function in post-ACL surgical female athletes.

### Study Arms

| Arm | Intervention |
|---|---|
| **EXPHMB (Experimental)** | 3g CaHMB + 2000 IU Vitamin D3/day, split into 2 doses, starting 2 weeks pre-surgery through return-to-sport clearance |
| **CONPLA (Placebo)** | 510mg Calcium Lactate capsules/day, same dosing schedule |

> Doses are consumed 60 min before and immediately after each PT session. On non-PT days, doses are taken in the morning and ~90 min later.

### Key Outcomes

**Primary Outcomes (measured every 6 weeks for 9 months):**
- Change in leg lean muscle mass (absolute kg via BIA)
- Change in muscle cross-sectional area (cm²) via ultrasound
- Percent change in cross-sectional area of quadriceps and hamstrings

**Secondary Outcomes (measured every 3 months for 9 months):**
- Isometric leg strength change (MVIC for leg extension and leg curl) relative to contralateral limb
- Change in EMG amplitude (RMS)

**Exploratory Outcomes:**
- Body composition (ALM, fat mass, total lean mass)
- Functional performance asymmetry index (drop landing, vertical jump, single/triple/cross-over hop, figure-8 hop, leg press reps)

### Assessment Timeline

| Timepoint | Assessments |
|---|---|
| **T0 (Pre-surgery)** | Body composition, ultrasound, strength, food/exercise logs |
| **T1 (2 weeks post-op)** | Body composition, ultrasound |
| **T2–T5 (every 6 weeks)** | Body composition, ultrasound, strength (when cleared) |
| **TRTS (Return to Sport)** | Full assessment battery + food/exercise logs |

### Eligibility Summary
- Biological female, ages 18–45
- ACL injury requiring reconstructive knee surgery
- Intends to complete post-op rehab at affiliated PT clinic
- No recent supplement use (except multivitamins), no prior knee surgery in operative limb, not pregnant

## Trial 3 — Plyometric Training ± Hamstring Strengthening for Preventing ACL Injuries in Female Amateur Athletes {#trial-3}

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | [NCT07047833](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT07047833) |
| **Status** | ✅ Completed |
| **Sponsor** | University of Lahore |
| **PI** | Fareeha Raja |
| **Study Dates** | May 2024 – March 2025 |
| **Enrollment** | 60 female participants (ages 18–30) |
| **Design** | Randomized controlled trial, single-blind (outcomes assessor) |
| **Location** | University of Lahore Teaching Hospital, Lahore, Pakistan |

### Background & Rationale
ACL injuries are prevalent in sports requiring jumping, cutting, and pivoting. Female athletes have well-documented anatomical and biomechanical vulnerabilities. This trial tested whether adding **hamstring strengthening** to a standard **plyometric training program** provides greater ACL injury prevention benefits in female amateur athletes compared to plyometric training alone.

### Study Arms

| Group | Protocol | Session Duration | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Group A (Experimental)** | Plyometric training + Hamstring strengthening | 60 min | 2x/week × 6 weeks |
| **Group B (Control)** | Plyometric training only | 40 min | 2x/week × 6 weeks |

**Group A Exercise Details:**
- *Plyometrics:* Squat jumps, depth jumps, lateral jumps
- *Hamstring strengthening:* Nordic hamstring curls, machine-based leg curls
- *Warm-up/cool-down:* 15-min warm-up + 5-min cool-down (standardized)

**Group B Exercise Details:**
- *Plyometrics:* Squat jumps, depth jumps, lateral jumps (progressive intensity)
- *Warm-up/cool-down:* 15-min warm-up + 5-min cool-down (standardized)

### Key Outcomes

**Primary Outcome:**
- **Knee flexion and extension strength** — measured via handheld dynamometer (kg) at Baseline, Week 3, and Week 6

**Secondary Outcomes:**
- **Dynamic balance** — Star Excursion Balance Test (SEBT), multi-directional reach distances
- **Functional mobility** — Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS), 5 subscales: Pain, Symptoms, ADL, Sport/Rec, Quality of Life (0–100 scale; higher = better)

> 📌 **Key Insight:** Both groups trained 2x/week over 6 weeks, but the combined plyometric + hamstring group received an additional 20 min of targeted hamstring work per session. This design isolates the additive contribution of hamstring strengthening to ACL injury prevention metrics.

### Eligibility Summary
- Female amateur athletes, ages 18–30
- Sports involving jumping, cutting, pivoting (e.g., basketball, soccer)
- Training frequency ≥6–8 sessions/week including competition
- No prior muscle/joint injury history, no prior pre-operative rehab program

## Cross-Trial Key Takeaways

### 🔑 Common Themes Across All Three Trials

1. **Female athletes are a distinct high-risk population.** All three trials explicitly recognize the elevated ACL injury risk in females driven by hormonal, anatomical, biomechanical, and neuromuscular factors.

2. **Supervised, multi-component rehab outperforms minimal intervention.** The Mayo Clinic trial (NCT03190889) showed that home-only programs (25% re-injury rate) significantly underperform compared to supervised protocols (~11–16%).

3. **Hamstring strength is consistently central.** Whether through neuromuscular training (TNMT), supplementation to prevent hamstring atrophy (HMB trial), or direct hamstring strengthening exercises (Lahore trial), all three trials converge on hamstring function as a key modifiable factor.

4. **Plyometric and neuromuscular training are foundational.** Jump training, agility drills, and neuromuscular control exercises appear in all three intervention frameworks.

5. **Muscle atrophy post-surgery is a critical gap.** The HMB trial (NCT05869812) uniquely addresses the nutritional/supplementation dimension of recovery — an often overlooked factor in standard rehab protocols.

6. **Return-to-sport criteria should be criteria-based, not time-based.** The Mayo Clinic trial's risk stratification model supports individualized, biomechanics-informed return-to-sport decisions.

## Rehabilitation Protocol Quick Reference

### Phase-by-Phase Framework (synthesized from trial data)

| Phase | Timeframe | Focus Areas | Key Exercises |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-op** | Pre-surgery | Reduce swelling, maintain strength | Quad sets, SLR, range of motion |
| **Early Post-op** | Weeks 1–6 | Muscle preservation, reduce atrophy | MVIC, BIA monitoring, HMB supplementation (if applicable) |
| **Intermediate** | Weeks 6–16 | Strength symmetry, balance | Leg press, hamstring curls (Nordic), SEBT balance drills |
| **Late / Neuromuscular** | Weeks 12–24 | Neuromuscular control, sport mechanics | Plyometrics (squat jumps, depth jumps, lateral jumps), biofeedback drills, single-leg drills |
| **Return-to-Sport** | Months 6–12+ | Sport-specific drills, re-injury prevention | Cutting/pivoting drills, agility ladders, sport simulation |

### ⚠️ Red Flags for Elevated Re-Injury Risk (from NCT03190889)
- Net internal rotation moment of the **uninvolved hip**
- Increased total **frontal plane knee movement**
- Greater asymmetry in **internal knee extensor moment** at initial contact
- Deficits in **single-leg postural stability** of the involved limb

> These parameters predicted second ACL injury with **sensitivity 0.92 / specificity 0.88** in the Mayo Clinic cohort.
