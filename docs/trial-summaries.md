## Trial 1 — NCT07128602

### 🧠 Non-invasive Brain Stimulation (tDCS) to Improve Quadriceps Function After ACL Reconstruction

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT07128602 |
| **Status** | 🟢 Recruiting |
| **Phase** | N/A (Device Study) |
| **Sponsor** | Arcadia University |
| **Funder** | NIH / Eunice Kennedy Shriver NICHD (Grant: 1R15HD118518-01) |
| **PI** | Ryan Zarzycki, PT, PhD (Associate Professor, Arcadia University) |
| **Location** | Glenside, Pennsylvania, USA |
| **Start Date** | June 2025 |
| **Est. Completion** | March 2028 |
| **Enrollment** | 42 females (estimated) |
| **Age Range** | 18–35 years |
| **Sex** | Female only |

#### 🎯 Study Objective
To determine whether anodal transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) — a non-invasive brain stimulation technique — can improve quadriceps muscle performance in women 2–6 months after ACL reconstruction by targeting the neural drive deficits (reduced corticospinal excitability) that standard rehab protocols fail to address.

#### 🔑 Key Background Finding
Prior work by the PI (Leung et al., 2024, *J Orthop Res*) showed that **females recovering from ACLR have more severe corticospinal excitability deficits and quadriceps impairments than males**, making this a female-specific neurological recovery gap.

#### 🧪 Design
- Randomized, **triple-blinded** parallel RCT
- **Active tDCS arm:** 20 min of anodal tDCS over the motor cortex contralateral to the surgical limb during quadriceps torque-matching task (6 sessions)
- **Sham tDCS arm:** Device active for first and last 30 seconds only

#### 📏 Primary Outcomes
- Peak quadriceps torque (isokinetic dynamometer, MVIC at 90° hip / 60° knee flexion)
- Corticospinal excitability — slope of TMS-induced stimulus-response curve

#### 📏 Secondary Outcomes
- Rate of torque development (RTD 0–100ms, 100–200ms)
- Active motor threshold (TMS)

#### 💡 Clinical Significance
This is the **first female-specific neurological ACLR rehab trial** to test brain stimulation as a targeted adjunct to standard physiotherapy. If successful, tDCS could become a standard tool in post-ACLR quad rehabilitation for women.

## Trial 2 — NCT05527171

### 🥽 Virtual Reality Mindfulness Meditation After ACL Reconstruction

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT05527171 |
| **Status** | 🟢 Recruiting |
| **Phase** | N/A (Behavioral Study) |
| **Sponsor** | University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill |
| **Funder** | NIH / NIAMS (Grant: K23AR079056) |
| **PI** | Shelby E. Baez, PhD, ATC |
| **Location** | Chapel Hill, North Carolina, USA |
| **Start Date** | September 2023 |
| **Est. Completion** | June 2026 |
| **Enrollment** | 48 females (estimated) |
| **Age Range** | 14–25 years |
| **Sex** | Female only |

#### 🎯 Study Objective
To evaluate whether an 8-week virtual reality (VR) mindfulness meditation program reduces injury-related fear (kinesiophobia), improves jump-landing biomechanics, and normalizes brain activity in female athletes 6 months to 10 years post-ACLR.

#### 🔑 Key Background Finding
Injury-related fear is a major driver of **reduced return-to-sport rates, decreased physical activity, and ACL re-injury** — yet standard rehabilitation does not address it. VR mindfulness has demonstrated superior mindfulness state induction vs. traditional meditation.

#### 🧪 Design
- Randomized, **double-blinded** parallel RCT
- **VR Mindfulness arm:** 8 weeks (3×/week) of guided VR mindfulness meditation + advanced neuromuscular training
- **VR Sham arm:** 8 weeks (3×/week) of passive VR immersion (no meditation) + advanced neuromuscular training

#### 📏 Primary Outcomes
- Injury-related fear (Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia-11; scores 11–44, higher = more fear)
- Feasibility: recruitment rate, retention rate, acceptability survey score

#### 📏 Secondary Outcomes
- External knee abduction moment (3D motion capture + force plates during jump-landing)
- Knee flexion excursion during jump-landing
- Brain activity — % BOLD fMRI signal in Default Mode Network during sport-specific vs. ADL imagery

#### 💡 Clinical Significance
This trial directly addresses the **psychological barrier to return-to-sport** in female athletes — a dimension almost entirely overlooked in standard ACL rehab. VR-based delivery makes it scalable and clinician-friendly.

## Trial 3 — NCT07047833

### 🏋️ Plyometric Training With or Without Hamstring Strengthening for ACL Injury Prevention in Female Amateur Athletes

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT07047833 |
| **Status** | ✅ Completed |
| **Phase** | N/A (Behavioral RCT) |
| **Sponsor** | University of Lahore, Pakistan |
| **PI** | Fareeha Raja |
| **Location** | University of Lahore Teaching Hospital, Lahore, Pakistan |
| **Start Date** | May 2024 |
| **Completion Date** | March 2025 |
| **Enrollment** | 60 female athletes (actual) |
| **Age Range** | 18–30 years |
| **Sex** | Female only |
| **Sports** | Basketball, soccer (jumping/cutting/pivoting) |

#### 🎯 Study Objective
To determine whether adding targeted hamstring strengthening to plyometric training provides greater ACL injury prevention benefits than plyometric training alone in female amateur athletes.

#### 🧪 Design
- Randomized, single-blind (outcomes assessor blinded) parallel RCT
- **Group A (n=30):** Plyometric + hamstring strengthening — 2×/week, 60-min sessions, 6 weeks
  - Plyometrics: squat jumps, depth jumps, lateral jumps
  - Hamstrings: Nordic hamstring curls, machine-based leg curls
- **Group B (n=30):** Plyometric only — 2×/week, 40-min sessions, 6 weeks
- Assessments at **Baseline, Week 3, Week 6**

#### 📏 Primary Outcomes
- Knee flexion and extension strength (handheld dynamometer, kg)

#### 📏 Secondary Outcomes
- Dynamic balance (Star Excursion Balance Test — SEBT)
- Functional mobility (KOOS — Knee Injury & Osteoarthritis Outcome Score; 5 subscales, 0–100)

#### 💡 Clinical Significance
Provides direct evidence for the **additive value of hamstring co-strengthening** in female ACL prevention programs. Hamstring weakness is a known sex-specific ACL risk factor; this trial quantifies the effect size of addressing it alongside plyometric work.

## Trial 4 — NCT07589309

### 🩸 Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) ± Total Motion Release (TMR) in Post-operative ACL Rehabilitation

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT07589309 |
| **Status** | ✅ Completed |
| **Phase** | N/A (Device + Procedure RCT) |
| **Sponsor** | Green International University, Pakistan |
| **PI** | Ali Muslim Raza |
| **Collaborator** | Sir Ganga Ram Hospital |
| **Location** | Lahore, Punjab, Pakistan |
| **Start Date** | November 2025 |
| **Completion Date** | May 2026 |
| **Enrollment** | 44 participants (actual) |
| **Age Range** | 16–45 years |
| **Sex** | Both male and female |
| **Graft Type** | Hamstring or patellar tendon autograft |

#### 🎯 Study Objective
To compare BFR training alone vs. BFR + Total Motion Release (TMR) in restoring quadriceps strength, balance, and functional knee scores in patients 2–4 weeks after primary unilateral ACLR.

#### 🧪 Design
- Randomized, **triple-blinded** parallel RCT (participant, care provider, outcomes assessor)
- **BFR + TMR Group:** 3×/week, 6 months
  - BFR: inflatable cuff at 60–80% limb occlusion pressure; low-load exercises (mini squats, knee extensions)
  - TMR: trunk rotations, side bending, hip shifts on the **contralateral (non-painful) side** before BFR
- **BFR Only Group:** Same BFR protocol without TMR
- Assessments at **Baseline, 6 weeks, 12 weeks, 6 months**

#### 📏 Primary Outcomes
- Maximum isometric lower limb strength (hand-held dynamometer)
- Single Leg Stance (SLS) test duration
- IKDC Subjective Knee Form score (0–100; higher = better function)

#### 💡 Clinical Significance
TMR is a novel **movement symmetry technique** applied to the contralateral limb to influence the injured side neuromotor control. This trial tests whether addressing central neuromotor asymmetry alongside peripheral strength (BFR) accelerates functional recovery — directly relevant for female athletes returning to sport.

## Trial 5 — NCT01591941

### ⚽ Core-PAC Warm-up to Reduce ACL Biomechanical Risk Factors in Female Youth Soccer Players

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT01591941 |
| **Status** | ✅ Completed |
| **Phase** | Phase 2 |
| **Sponsor** | University of British Columbia, Canada |
| **PI** | Susan R. Harris, PhD |
| **Location** | GF Strong Rehab Centre, Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada |
| **Start Date** | June 2006 |
| **Completion Date** | August 2007 |
| **Enrollment** | 20 females (actual) |
| **Age Range** | 14–17 years |
| **Sex** | Female only |
| **Sport** | Soccer |

#### 🎯 Study Objective
To evaluate whether a Core Position and Control movement strategy (Core-PAC) warm-up program reduces biomechanical ACL injury risk factors (knee abduction moments, reduced flexion angles) in female youth soccer players.

#### 🔑 Key Mechanism
The Core-PAC strategy moves the **centre of mass (COM) closer to the base of support (BOS)** during cutting movements, reducing loading from frontal/transverse planes (where female athletes are most vulnerable) toward the safer sagittal plane.

#### 🧪 Design
- Randomized, single-blind (outcomes assessor blinded) parallel RCT — Phase 2
- **Core-PAC group:** 6-week Core-PAC warm-up before soccer training (trunk-first movement initiation)
- **Control group:** Standard soccer warm-up
- Biomechanical testing at 6 weeks (GF Strong Rehab Centre motion lab)

#### 📏 Primary Outcomes
- Peak knee flexion angle during side-cut, unanticipated side-cut, and side-hop tasks
- Peak knee abduction moment during the same tasks

#### 💡 Clinical Significance
One of the **earliest Phase 2 RCTs** specifically targeting female youth ACL biomechanics. The Core-PAC concept (trunk-led movement) has influenced modern warm-up programs like FIFA 11+ and forms the foundation of neuromuscular ACL prevention curricula.
